- **Viral expert posts and comments** — not marketing, but voiced by experts/founders

## Comment types

### Type 1: Reply on your own post

We respond to comments on the author's own post.

**Rules:**
- Reply within the first **30 minutes** (critical for the algorithm)
- At least **15 words** (longer comments = more weight)
- Add value, not just "Thanks!"
- Ask a follow-up question (drives the thread)
- Tone = author's DIP (the same voice as in the post)

**Formulas:**

| Situation | Formula | Example (Seva's tone) |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| Agreement | Expand + Add layer | "Exactly. And what most people miss is [deeper insight]. We saw this when [specific example]." |
| Question | Answer + Bridge | "Great question. The short answer: [answer]. The real answer: [nuance]. I actually wrote about this in [context]." |
| Disagreement | Acknowledge + Reframe | "I see your point, but in our experience [counter-evidence]. The nuance is [specific difference]." |
| Compliment | Redirect to insight | "Appreciate that. The real credit goes to [team/system/process]. What made it work was [specific detail]." |
| "How can I learn more?" | Give value + soft CTA | "Here's what I'd start with: [actionable tip]. If you want the full breakdown, DM me — happy to share." |

---

### Type 2: Comment on someone else's post

We comment on posts by other people in the author's niche — for visibility, networking, algorithm training.

**Rules:**
- Only posts in the author's niche (DIP Expertise Clusters)
- At least **15 words** (meaty comment)
- Add a unique perspective from the author's experience
- No generic "Great post!" or "So true!"
- Don't sell, don't pitch
- Tone = DIP, but a bit softer (we're a guest)

**Formulas:**

| Situation | Formula | Example (Kirill's tone) |
|----------|---------|---------------------|
| Add your own experience | "We did something similar + [result]" | "We tested exactly this with one of our clients. The surprising part: [unexpected finding]. [Specific number] confirmed it." |
| Clarify/extend | "This + [missing piece]" | "One thing I'd add: [insight]. When we ran this for [client type], the difference was [specific metric]." |
| Ask a smart question | "Curious about [specific aspect]" | "Curious — did you see the same pattern with [specific variation]? In our case, [what happened] was the opposite." |
| Share a contrarian position | "Interesting — our data shows [different]" | "Our data actually shows the opposite for [specific segment]. Could be industry-specific though — what's your sample look like?" |

**What to NEVER do:**
- "Great post!" / "Love this!" / "So true!" (empty comments)
- "We do this too, check out [product link]" (spam)
- Long monologues unrelated to the original post
- Commenting for the sake of commenting (the algorithm detects patterns)

### Type 3: Initiating a discussion

The first comment under your own post (to direct the discussion).

**Rules:**
- Post within the first 5 minutes after the post goes live
- Ask a directing question or add a bonus insight
- Don't repeat the post's content
- No links (even in the first comment — penalized as of 2026)

**Formulas:**

| Formula | Example |
|---------|--------|
| Bonus insight | "One thing I didn't include in the post: [additional insight]. Curious if anyone has seen this too." |
| Directing question | "Biggest question I get about this: [question]. Here's my take: [brief answer]. What's yours?" |
| Behind the scenes | "Story behind this post: [how the idea came to me]. The original version was very different." |

## STEP-BY-STEP: Creating a comment

### Step 1: Identify the author and load the DIP

1. Who is commenting? → Load `[Author]-DIP.md`
2. Memorize:
   - Tone of Voice (how they speak)
   - Speech Patterns (signature phrases)
   - "They NEVER" (what to avoid)
   - Expertise Clusters (which topics they can comment on authoritatively)

### Step 2: Analyze the context

1. Read the original post / comment we are replying to
2. Determine:
   - What is the post about? Which of the author's expertise clusters does it map to?
   - Is there room to add a unique perspective?
   - What tone is appropriate? (serious, light, provocative)

### Step 3: Pick a formula

1. Determine the comment type (Reply / On someone else's post / Initiation)
2. Pick a formula from the tables above
3. Adapt to the author's DIP

### Step 4: Write the comment

**Rules:**
- At least 15 words (for algorithmic weight)
- Optimally 30–80 words (substantial but not an essay)
- First person (I/my)
- Specifics from the author's experience (DIP Stories Bank, metrics)
- No hashtags in comments
- No emojis (unless the author uses them in the DIP)

### Step 4.5: Source Grounding (CRITICAL)

> Comments follow the same fact standards as posts (Rule 6). Every claim in a comment must be **traceable** to a real source. If there is no source — don't assert. Ask a question, acknowledge a limitation, or stay silent.

**Process:**

1. **Reread the draft comment** — flag every factual claim
2. **For each claim find a source:**
   - DIP Stories Bank → a specific case with details
   - DIP Expertise Clusters → a confirmed topic for the author
   - A real post by the author → a specific metric / fact (remember: posts = OUTPUT; quote with care)
   - Transcript → direct quote or paraphrase
3. **Classify each claim:**

| Category | What to do | Example |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Verified fact** — there is a concrete source with details | Keep as is | "Creative kill threshold at $15" (DIP Stories Bank, post 2026-01-13) |
| **Grounded inference** — a direct corollary of verified facts | Keep, but don't embellish | "Delayed conversions distort early ROAS" (DIP Cluster 2: attribution challenges) |
| **Thin ground** — the author's topic, but no specific case | Reframe: ask a question or refer to a known fact without inventing | Instead of "We've been hit by model downtimes" → "reliability of underlying infra matters more than we expected when we started" |
| **Bullshit** — sounds plausible but is invented | Delete or replace with a question | Instead of "I've watched this in agencies for 20 years" (if there is no concrete case) → ask the commenter a question |

4. **If you don't have a concrete case on the topic — three tactics:**
   - **Ask a question** — shows interest, drives the thread, requires no facts ("Curious how you'd frame predictable cost as a product feature — flat rate? Usage caps?")
   - **Refer to a known fact** without invention ("We run 4 layers of agents in production right now" — verified, post 2026-02-11)
   - **Acknowledge someone else's idea and add your own framework** ("Your 'six weeks instead of six months' framing is accurate — speed without strategy clarity just means faster failure cycles" — uses the known position "strategy stays human")

**Red flag:** If more than 50% of the claims in a comment fall into "Thin ground" or "Bullshit" — rewrite the comment. A short comment with one verified fact beats a long one with three inventions.

#### Source Grounding: Live example (Seva, "Vibe Coding & B2B SaaS" post, March 2026)

Three commenters proposed additions to Seva's 5-point list. Below — the grounding process for each reply.

**Comment: "Add predictable uptime and cost as 6th category"**

| Claim in the draft | Category | Source | Decision |
|------------------------|-----------|----------|---------|
| "We run 4 layers of agents in production" | Verified fact | Agent Layers post, 2026-02-11 | Keep |
| "reliability matters more than we expected" | Grounded inference | Corollary of running agents in production | Keep |
| ~~"We've been hit by model downtimes that broke real workflows"~~ | Bullshit | No specific case in DIP/transcripts | Delete |
| "Curious how you'd frame predictable cost as a product feature?" | Question (not a claim) | N/A | Keep — drives the thread |

**Result:**

> Makes sense as a separate category. We run 4 layers of agents in production right now — and reliability of the underlying infra matters more than we expected when we started. On cost: agree, token-based pricing is confusing for anyone outside of engineering. Curious how you'd frame "predictable cost" as a product feature — flat rate? Usage caps? Something else?

*Why it works: one verified fact (4 layers) + one grounded inference (reliability matters) + a question instead of an invention. Short but real.*

---

**Comment: "The answer might be: ability to know what to build, not just build it fast"**

| Claim in the draft | Category | Source | Decision |
|------------------------|-----------|----------|---------|
| ~~"I've watched the re-translation problem for 20 years in agencies"~~ | Thin ground | 20 years in agencies — verified. But no specific "intent distorts through layers" case in the transcripts | Remove the specific claim about re-translation |

**Result:**

We've been saying "strategy stays human, execution becomes effortless" — but your point sharpens it: if the original intent is wrong, faster execution just means faster failure. The question "which specific bet does your product actually test" — that's the one I keep coming back to with our own roadmap.

*Why it works: leans on Seva's known formulation + a logical corollary from it. Acknowledges someone else's idea ("your point sharpens it") without pretending "I've seen this too."*

**Comment: "I'd add a 6th category: domain expertise embedded in the product"**

| Claim in the draft | Category | Source | Decision |
|------------------------|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Creative kill thresholds — when to stop at $15 spend" | Verified fact | DIP Stories Bank, post 2026-01-13 (1,203 creatives, +$5,000/month) | Keep |

You can vibe-code the dashboard for that, but the logic underneath — which patterns burn out, how delayed conversions distort early ROAS — that comes from running campaigns across dozens of clients. Tooling commoditizes. That knowledge layer doesn't.

*Why it works: the "meatiest" reply — two verified facts ($15 threshold + dozens of clients) + one grounded inference (delayed conversions). Every claim is traceable.*

## Iron rules (from practice, updated 2026-03-02)

### 1. ALWAYS reply to comments

Never "just a like". Even to a simple "Congrats!" — a short reply.

**IMPORTANT:** Do NOT repeat the same formula ("Thank you! [detail]") on every comment. Variety = a living profile. Same-y replies = a bot.

Every reply = a signal to the algorithm + respect for the person.

#### Bank of micro-replies to congratulations (mix & match)

**Energetic / forward-looking:**
- "Buckle up — this is just the beginning 😎"
- "The fun part starts now 🔥"
- "Stay tuned 🚀"
- "This is just chapter one."
- "Best is ahead!"
- "We're just warming up."

**Warm / grateful (without generic "Thank you!"):**
- "Means a lot 🙏"
- "Appreciate you!"
- "This made my day."
- "❤️"
- "🙏🔥"

**With character / playful:**
- "😎🚀"
- "🤝"
- "LFG 🚀"
- "Not gonna lie — feels good."
- "Now the real work begins 💪"

**Minimalist (when you've already replied a lot):**
- "🙏"
- "🚀"
- "💪"
- "❤️🔥"
- Just an emoji — also OK (better than a copy-paste "Thank you!")

**With specifics (when appropriate):**
- "Thank you! [one specific detail about the person or context]"
- "[Name], appreciate the support — especially from someone who [knows the space / has been through this]."

**Rotation rule:** Don't use the same formula twice in a row. Alternate: emoji → words → forward-looking → warm → playful. If a post has 10+ congratulations — 3–4 can be just emojis, the rest — words.

### 2. Don't duplicate the post's content

**FORBIDDEN** to quote metrics, phrases, or formulations from the post back to the author. That's a lazy comment and looks AI-generated.

- NO: "The '10-15 minutes instead of an hour' — that number..." (a number from the author's post)
- NO: "I've seen what that looks like on the inside" (generic bullshit)
- YES: Your own experience, your own observation, your own angle — without retelling what the author already wrote

Exception: if the quoting brings a **genuinely unique angle** (not praising the quote, but contrast, addition, different context).

### 3. Address directly (2nd person)

On a colleague's post — write TO the person, not ABOUT them:

### 4. No "not X but Y" construction

Forbidden constructions like: "That shift never happens because of a dashboard... It happens when..."
That sounds preachy and templated. Say it directly.

Also forbidden are **mini-contrasts inside facts**: "Not because we asked — because the results forced it." It's the same construction, just shorter. The fact is strong on its own — no need to amplify it through negation. Just state the fact.
- NO: "Teams put it into OKRs. Not because we asked — because the results forced it."
- YES: "Teams put it into OKRs. The momentum is real."

Also forbidden are **transformational transitions**: "it stops being X and becomes Y", "it's not a feature, it's infrastructure", "this isn't X — this is Y". Same construction, disguised as insight. Sounds generic and empty. Just say what it is and why it matters.
- NO: "it stops being a feature and becomes infrastructure"
- NO: "it's not a dashboard, it's a decision engine"
- YES: A concrete fact or observation without renaming

### 5. No self-promotion on others' posts

When commenting on a colleague's post — **support the colleague**. Don't pull the focus to your own story.
- NO: Seva talks about his agency in a comment under Natasha's post
- YES: Seva says something concrete about Natasha and her contribution

### 6. Same fact standards as in posts

Comments = same rules as posts. Don't invent experience; check context against the DIP and transcripts.
- NO: "I remember the first client setup" (if Kirill doesn't remember it)
- NO: "One of our pilots said..." (if there is no real quote)
- YES: Only what really happened and is confirmed

### 7. Plurio — voice rules

- Plurio speaks **in the first person "I"** — never "our agent", never third person
- Plurio = **a positive Marvin** (Hitchhiker's Guide) — dry wit, self-aware, confident
- **No self-deprecation** — there was no "at my worst", "early pilots, false positives". Plurio worked well from day one.
- **No "my job is to..."** — Plurio is not a human with job responsibilities
- **No "we'd rather"** — the agent has no corporate-style preferences

### 8. Filler words

Do NOT use:
- "I've seen what that looks like" / "I've seen this firsthand"
- "This needed to be said"
- "That carries weight"
- Any abstract validations without specifics

### 9. Caution with relationships

- "my friend" — only if you're **certain** that this person is a friend
- Don't assign relationships that may not exist

### 11. AI-detectable comment structure

**Mini-essay structure (thesis → development → conclusion)** is the main AI marker in comments. Living comments = one thought, sometimes uneven, sometimes cut off. An AI comment = a perfectly built argument of 3–4 sentences with logical transitions.

**Signs:**
- Three sentences, each developing the previous one
- Polished metaphors ("more craft than engineering") — a live person doesn't polish metaphors in comments
- Forced casual opener ("yeah", "right", "so") + then structured text = AI trying to look casual
- Declare → explain → conclude pattern

**Rule:** One thought. Can be uneven. No conclusion needed. No transition needed. If a comment looks like a mini-post — it's AI.

### 12. Team: each author = unique angle

When multiple authors comment on the same post — the angles **do not overlap**:
- Ella: warm, human, about the team
- Seva: vision, strategic acknowledgment
- Kirill: product, hands-on, specifics
- Plurio: agent POV, dry wit, self-aware

## Anti-Patterns (what not to do)

| Pattern | Why it's bad | Use instead |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| "Great post!" / "Love this!" | Zero value, empty signal | Add a concrete example from your experience |
| **Generic validation phrases** ("This is the shift", "This is the line", "Exactly — and...", "100%", "So true") | Sounds like empty agreement. Adds no value. Obvious and trite | **Instead of validation — your own concrete experience.** Not "this is great" → but "we ran this with a client and [what happened]". Not "exactly" → but a concrete example that confirms the author's idea |
| **Quoting a line from the post + "this is the line/shift/key"** | Lazy comment. Repeats the post instead of adding something new. Looks AI-generated | If you want to reference the post — add YOUR experience that confirms or extends the idea. A real story > praising the quote |
| **Retelling metrics/phrases from the post back to the author** | The author already wrote it. Looks like an echo. | Either your own angle on that metric, or a different fact |
| **"I've seen..." / "I've watched..."** without specifics | Generic bullshit. Means nothing. | A concrete fact or silence |
| **"Not X but Y" construction** (including mini-versions: "Not because X — because Y") | Preachy template. Even the short form ("not because we asked — because results") — same construction | Speak directly, without contrast. The fact is strong on its own — don't amplify through negation |
| **Transformational transitions** ("it stops being X and becomes Y", "it's not a feature, it's infrastructure", "this isn't X — this is Y") | Disguised "not X but Y". Sounds generic, creates the illusion of insight without real substance | A concrete fact or observation. Don't rename — say what it is and why it matters |
| **Self-promotion on someone else's post** | Pulls attention away from the author | Support the author, add their angle |
| **"my friend" without certainty** | May be inappropriate | Address by name |
| Copy-paste of one comment into different posts | LinkedIn detects the pattern | Every comment is unique |
| Comments every 5 minutes in a row | Looks like a bot | 5–10 per hour, with pauses |
| Comments only within the team's circle | Engagement pod pattern | Mix: 30% team, 70% external |
| Selling in comments | Spam, negative signal | Only value, DM to continue |
| **Pitch tone in comments on congratulatory posts** (investors, partners, colleagues) | An investor/partner already supported you — no need to sell back to them. Feels out of place | Gratitude + fact (without sales framing). Metrics OK, but as "here's what's happening" rather than "here's why we're great" |
| **"That's exactly how we designed it" / "That's exactly how I think"** and any variants ("this is exactly what we...", "that's precisely our approach") | Instantly gives away an AI author. Echo validation: you repeat the commenter's idea and say "we think the same". Zero value | Instead of "we think the same" — **a concrete fact** from your experience. Don't confirm someone's thought with words — confirm with action or result |
| "Link in comments" under your own post | Penalized as of 2026 | Value in the post; if the link is really needed — in the body |
| **Quoting a COMMENTER's phrase in quotes + "is key/important"** ("the 'already working' part is key") | AI pattern #1. Pulling a piece of someone else's sentence, putting it in quotes, declaring it "key". Live people don't dissect each other's phrases into quotes in comments | React to the thought, not the formulation. Show it through your own example, not through "your 'X' is key" |
| **Prepared punchline formulas** ("broken process + agent = broken process at scale", "X + Y = Z") | Formulaic construction, sounds like a pre-written line, not a spontaneous comment | Say the thought directly, without a formula |
| **Sweeping "most people/teams" generalizations** ("most teams skip that", "most people rush past this") | Generalization without specifics. Who are these "most people"? Empty claim | A concrete example or cut it. If there is no example — don't generalize |
| **Evaluative language** ("was great on this one", "made it special") | Sounds like a jury rating, not a peer/colleague reacting | Reaction, not evaluation. "You'll love it" vs "was great on this one" |
| **CTA endings in comments** ("Worth a watch", "Check it out", "Highly recommend") | A comment turns into an ad. Live people don't end comments with a call to action | End with a thought. Without a call to action |
| **AI transition patterns** ("But the X changes things", "this is where Y comes in", "But the feedback loop changes things") | Template transitions that instantly read as AI authorship | Just the next sentence. No clichéd transition |
| **Vulnerability oversharing** ("we're stuck", "this is where we struggle", "honestly — X is where we're stuck right now") | A founder won't publicly write "we're stuck" in a LinkedIn comment. Strange and out of place | If the topic is hard — show it through specifics ("we've been iterating on this"), not through "we're stuck" |
| **Thematic mismatch** (a convenient example from the DIP unrelated to the topic of the conversation) | A conversation about brand → a reply about creative kill thresholds. The other person sees the swap. Means the author wasn't listening and inserted a template | Stay on the topic of the conversation. If there is no on-topic example — a short reply without an example beats a long one off-topic |
