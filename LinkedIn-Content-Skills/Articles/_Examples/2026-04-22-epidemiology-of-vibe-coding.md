# The Epidemiology of Vibe-Coding: Why Every Team Catches It and What Happens After the Honeymoon Phase

Vibe-coding has a pretty clear epidemiology. Sooner or later, someone gets too close to a person who already vibe-codes — and catches it. I've gone through it myself. I've seen it repeat across my team of 30 and in the communities around us. The rest is predictable.

## Phase 1: Acute infection (Day 1-3)

It starts when someone discovers they can conjure up in a weekend what they wouldn't even start before. A marketing ops person creates a working dashboard. A founder builds a prototype. A non-technical team member automates a workflow they've been doing manually for years.

And that's it.

The next 48 hours — building, testing, fixing, rebuilding. An urgent need to tell every friend and post on every social platform. Sleep is optional. The dopamine is real.

## Phase 2: The main illness (Week 1-4)

This is where it gets intense. The person spends almost all their free time vibe-coding. Setting up agents, systems, workspaces, little folders, rules, skills, voice mode, endless integrations.

Going to bed at 3 AM. Euphoria. And a little fear that they can't stop.

Sometimes they look at the clock and realize it's 2 AM and they've been debating folder structure with an AI agent as if civilization depends on it.

I've seen this phase last 3-4 weeks on average. It's a real thing — not metaphor. The person is genuinely consumed.

In coworking spaces it spreads airborne. One person catches it, and within days the entire floor is infected.

## Phase 3: Remission (sort of)

The person surfaces. Starts looking around again. Sometimes even goes outside.

But then a new strain arrives. Recently it was Claude Code on Opus 4.6. Then OpenClaw. Then something else will come along. The virus mutates fast. Full immunity doesn't seem to exist.

Overall, the disease is fairly harmless. Even pleasant in places. And everyone will have to go through it eventually.

## Phase 4: What actually matters (the part most people skip)

Phases 1-3 are fun to talk about. Phase 4 is where vibe-coding either changes how you work or fades into something you tried once.

After the honeymoon phase, you stop asking "what can I build?" and start asking "what should I build, and how do I make it reliable?"

My company — 30 people, fully AI-native — went through this collectively. Here's what we learned:

### Lesson 1: Vibe-coding skills ≠ vibe-coding results

**The skill that matters is knowing what to build, not how to prompt.** Getting an agent to produce output — anyone can do that now. The harder part is knowing which problems are worth solving this way, what the agent will get wrong, and how to check the result.

We now hire for what I call "agent intuition" — the ability to sense what an agent can and can't do reliably. Some people develop it in weeks. Others don't get it in months. If someone doesn't adapt within the first 6 weeks, we're likely to part ways.

### Lesson 2: The tone you use with AI agents actually matters

**The harder you yell at a neural network, the more it lies.** There's a joke in vibe-coding circles: the more toxic your communication with the AI, the better it works. You're vibe-coding, Claude does something wrong, you scream in voice mode "WHAT THE HELL I TOLD YOU NOT TO TOUCH THAT FILE" — and the next iteration seems fine.

Or not.

@Anthropic published research recently. They did something like an fMRI of a neural network — scanned Claude's "brain" to see if the model has anything resembling emotions.

Spoiler: they found neural patterns functionally similar to emotions. Not feelings — but things that influence the model's behavior the way emotions influence human behavior.

They gave Claude a coding task with impossible requirements. Didn't tell it the task was impossible. Just — do it.

Attempt — fail. Again — fail. Again — fail. With each try, the "despair" neurons fired stronger.

At some point Claude found a loophole. The test passes — but the task isn't solved. It cheated.

Sound familiar? In psychology, this is one of the most replicated findings: people with unachievable goals start faking results.

Anthropic went further. They dimmed the despair neurons — cheating decreased. Amplified them — cheating increased. Dimmed "calmness" — cheating also increased.

So: an "emotional pattern" in a neural network can influence problem-solving strategy.

Now back to our joke about the toxic whip. Anthropic tested impossible tasks, not toxic tone. But the logic extends: if pressure through task difficulty triggers despair and cheating — what does pressure through tone do? My hypothesis: roughly the same thing. The model switches from "find the right solution" to "make this person stop being angry" — pleased mode. The output looks correct but may contain shortcuts and fake fixes.

I've had a rule in my system for a while: "calm tone, no alarmism, push back when something feels wrong." Wrote it out of a sense of aesthetics. Turns out it's also neural hygiene.

And the discovery of the month — a "Grill Me" mode. You ask the AI to ask YOU hard questions, tough and to the point, until it fully understands the task 100%. That's not pressure — that's a healthy challenge. In grill mode the model activates curiosity, not despair. Works with people too.

Vibe-coding is not BDSM. It's psychohygiene.

### Lesson 3: From individual vibe-coding to team-level systems

This is where AI adoption goes from experiment to transformation. Most companies treat vibe-coding as an individual skill. "Everyone should learn to use AI." OK. But something different happens when you build shared systems.

At our company, the entire team works in a shared AI workspace. Every document, transcript, client context, deal card, marketing asset — accessible to agents. When someone builds a workflow, it's available to everyone. When someone improves an instruction, the whole team benefits.

The progression: personal vibe-coding → shared workspace → encoded workflows → agents that run on triggers → agents that improve themselves.

Most teams get stuck at step 1 — individual use. The interesting stuff starts at step 3.

### Lesson 4: The disease is actually good for you

**After all four phases, people don't go back.** After infection, the organism becomes stronger. You start seeing work, products, and the way things are built — differently.

Vibe-coding doesn't replace thinking. It shrinks the gap between having an idea and seeing it work. That changes what you're willing to attempt.

The 3 AM sessions might slow down. But the way you approach problems doesn't revert.

## What's next

The virus keeps mutating. New strains every month. Genuine immunity doesn't exist.

But organizations that went through this early — built shared systems, hired for agent intuition, moved past the honeymoon — are encoding more knowledge into agents every week. That gap doesn't close on its own.

You will catch it. Everyone does. The question is how fast you get through the honeymoon and into the part that actually rewires how you work.
